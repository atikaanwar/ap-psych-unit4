4.1 Attribution Theory & Person Perception

LTs 4.1-1 through 4.1-5 · How we perceive and explain others

Person perception: The process of forming impressions of others. We quickly judge people using limited information — thin slices of behavior often predict overall personality.

Attribution theory (Heider): We explain behavior as caused by internal (dispositional) factors — personality, ability, effort — or external (situational) factors — circumstances, luck, environment.

Three Key Biases — Know All Three
Fundamental Attribution Error (FAE): When judging others, we overestimate dispositional causes and underestimate situational causes.

Self-serving bias: We attribute our own successes to internal factors ("I'm smart") and failures to external ones ("The test was unfair") — protects self-esteem.

Actor-observer bias: We explain our own behavior situationally ("I was tired") but others' dispositionally ("She's always rude").

Social comparison (Festinger): We evaluate our opinions and abilities by comparing ourselves to others. Upward comparison (comparing to someone better) can motivate or depress; downward comparison (comparing to someone worse) boosts self-esteem. Shapes our self-perception profoundly.

Prejudice: An unjustified negative attitude toward a group and its members. Has three components: stereotype (cognitive belief), prejudice (emotional attitude), discrimination (behavioral action).

Explicit prejudice: Conscious, deliberate negative attitudes — the person is aware of and endorses the bias.

Implicit prejudice: Unconscious, automatic bias — measured by the Implicit Association Test (IAT), which reveals reaction-time differences when pairing concepts. People can hold implicit biases they sincerely deny holding.

Group distinctions that elicit prejudice:
• Race and ethnicity — most studied; linked to in-group bias and historical power structures
• Gender — sexism; gender stereotypes affect expectations and evaluations
• Age — ageism; stereotypes about the elderly and the young
• Sexual orientation — homophobia; often implicit even among those who explicitly report tolerance
• Physical appearance — attractiveness bias; "beautiful is good" heuristic

Social roots:
• Social identity theory (Tajfel & Turner): We categorize ourselves and others into groups, then favor the in-group to boost self-esteem. Even minimal group assignments ("red team") produce in-group bias.
• In-group vs. out-group: Out-group homogeneity — "they all look alike to us." In-group bias — favoritism toward own group.
• Conformity to social norms: We absorb prejudices from culture, family, and media.

Emotional roots:
• Scapegoat theory: When frustrated, people displace aggression onto a weaker out-group.
• Just-world phenomenon: Believing people get what they deserve leads to victim-blaming.

Cognitive roots:
• Categorization: We simplify the social world by grouping people; once grouped, we exaggerate between-group differences.
• Vivid cases: A single memorable out-group member confirms the stereotype (availability heuristic).
• Stereotype threat (Steele): Awareness of a negative stereotype disrupts performance — a self-fulfilling prophecy of prejudice.

4.2 Attitude Formation & Change

LTs 4.2-1 · 4.2-2 · How attitudes shape behavior and how we change them

Attitudes: Favorable or unfavorable evaluative reactions toward something or someone. Composed of:
• Cognitive: beliefs ("Smoking causes cancer")
• Affective: feelings ("I find smoking disgusting")
• Behavioral: tendency to act ("I avoid smokers")

Do attitudes predict behavior? Sometimes — when attitudes are specific to the behavior, strong, and formed through direct experience.

Do actions shape attitudes? Yes — this is the core of cognitive dissonance:

Cognitive Dissonance (Festinger, 1957)
When our actions contradict our attitudes, we experience psychological tension. To reduce it we either change our attitude to match our behavior, add new cognitions to justify the behavior, or change the behavior. Classic example: "I smoke, but smoking is unhealthy" → "Well, it helps me relax" (rationalization).

Foot-in-the-door: Once we comply with a small request, our self-image shifts ("I'm the kind of person who does this"), making larger compliance more likely.
Mere exposure effect: Repeated exposure to a stimulus — even without awareness — increases our liking of it.

The Elaboration Likelihood Model (Petty & Cacioppo) proposes two routes through which persuasion works:

FeatureCentral RoutePeripheral Route
ProcessCareful, logical analysis of argumentsSuperficial cues (attractiveness, authority)
AudienceMotivated and able to thinkDistracted or uninterested
ResultDurable attitude changeTemporary attitude change
ExampleReading a detailed policy briefBuying a product because a celebrity endorses it

Elements of effective persuasion:
• Source credibility: Expert, trustworthy communicators are more persuasive
• Message: Two-sided messages are more persuasive with informed audiences
• Channel: Face-to-face more persuasive than written for complex topics
• Audience: Young people and those with low self-esteem are more persuadable

4.3 Psychology of Social Situations

LTs 4.3-1 through 4.3-15 · Conformity, obedience, group behavior, aggression, attraction, altruism, conflict

Social contagion: Behaviors, emotions, and ideas spread through a group like a contagion — a form of automatic, unconscious conformity (laughter in a crowd, yawning, panic).

Conformity (Asch, 1951): ~37% of trials produced conformity even when the answer was clearly wrong. Conformity increases with: group unanimity, group size (up to 3–5), public responses, low self-confidence. One ally reduces conformity dramatically.

Normative influence: Conforming to fit in (public compliance, private disagreement).
Informational influence: Conforming because you believe the group has better information.

Obedience (Milgram, 1963): 65% administered maximum 450V shock. Obedience increased with: authority legitimacy, prestigious institution, victim at distance, no peer defiance model. Decreased with: victim proximity, peer refusal, no authority present.

What social influence studies teach us (LT 4.3-3)
These studies show that situational forces are far more powerful than we assume. We overestimate dispositional factors (the FAE) and underestimate how much context shapes behavior. BUT we also have power — a single dissenter breaks conformity; refusing to obey at lower levels prevents escalation.

Social facilitation (Zajonc): Others' presence creates arousal → enhances easy/well-practiced tasks, impairs difficult/novel tasks. Explains why athletes perform better in front of crowds on mastered skills.

Social loafing: Reduced effort in group tasks — accountability is diffused. More common in individualistic cultures; reduced by identifiable contributions.

Deindividuation: Loss of self-awareness and restraint when in a group or anonymous setting (online trolling, mob violence, costumed behavior). People feel less responsible for individual actions.

Group polarization: Discussion intensifies the group's average pre-existing attitude. Like-minded people become more extreme after talking — explains political echo chambers and online radicalization.

Groupthink (Janis): Cohesive groups prioritize harmony over critical thinking. Symptoms: illusion of invulnerability, collective rationalization, self-censorship, pressure on dissenters, illusion of unanimity. Prevention: assign devil's advocate, seek outside opinions, use anonymous input.

Culture shapes behavior through norms, values, and roles. Cross-cultural research shows both universal patterns and significant cultural variation.

Individualism vs. collectivism:
• Individualist (Western): Personal goals, independence, self-expression; attribute behavior dispositionally
• Collectivist (East Asian, Latin American): Group goals, interdependence, harmony; more situational attributions and stronger conformity

Norms: Unwritten rules about accepted behavior. Vary widely across cultures — what is polite in one culture is rude in another. Social roles (student, employee, parent) further shape behavior, sometimes overriding personal values (as Zimbardo's study showed).

Psychology's definition of aggression: Any physical or verbal behavior intended to hurt someone. Distinct from everyday usage — aggression is about intent, not just forcefulness.

Biological factors:
• Genetics: Heritability of temperament and impulse control
• Neural: Amygdala activation; reduced frontal lobe inhibition
• Biochemical: High testosterone correlates with dominance-seeking; low serotonin reduces impulse control; alcohol weakens frontal lobe inhibition

Psychological triggers (LT 4.3-9):
• Frustration-aggression principle (Dollard, 1939): Frustration (blocked goals) increases aggression, especially toward perceived sources
• Aversive events: Pain, heat, crowding, unpleasant smells all increase irritability
• Arousal transfer: Residual arousal from any source can intensify aggression

Social-cultural triggers:
• Observational learning: Bandura's Bobo doll; exposure to aggressive models increases aggression
• Media violence: Desensitizes; increases aggressive cognitions and behaviors (correlation + experimental evidence)
• Culture of honor: In some cultures, aggressive responses to perceived disrespect are normative

Factors predicting attraction (LT 4.3-10):
• Proximity: Mere exposure — we like what is familiar; geographic closeness predicts friendship and romance
• Physical attractiveness: "Beautiful is good" heuristic; physically attractive people are assumed to be more competent, happy, and successful; matching phenomenon — we tend to pair with those of similar attractiveness
• Similarity: We are attracted to those who share our values, interests, and backgrounds ("birds of a feather")
• Reciprocal liking: Knowing someone likes us increases our liking of them

How romantic love changes over time (LT 4.3-11):
• Passionate love: Intense, aroused state of longing; associated with arousal, idealization, novelty; peaks early in relationship
• Companionate love: Deep, affectionate attachment; based on trust, commitment, shared history; grows over time
• Equity and self-disclosure sustain long-term relationships; Sternberg's triangular theory adds intimacy, passion, and commitment as components

Altruism: Unselfish concern for the welfare of others. When are we most — and least — likely to help?

Bystander effect (Darley & Latané): As the number of witnesses increases, each person's likelihood of helping decreases. Mechanisms:
• Diffusion of responsibility: Each person feels less personally accountable
• Pluralistic ignorance: Looking to others for cues; others' inaction signals "no emergency"

Factors increasing helping: Alone (not in a crowd), unambiguous need, feeling similar to the victim, good mood, not in a hurry, smaller community.

Social exchange theory (LT 4.3-13): We help when the perceived benefits (social approval, reduced guilt, reciprocity) outweigh the costs (time, risk, effort). Helping is rational self-interest, not pure altruism.

Social norms that motivate helping:
• Reciprocity norm: Help those who have helped you
• Social responsibility norm: Help those who depend on you, even if they can't repay

Social traps (LT 4.3-14): Situations where individuals pursue self-interest in ways that are collectively destructive. Example: The Prisoner's Dilemma — both parties suffer if both defect, but individual incentive is to defect. Leads to arms races, pollution, depletion of shared resources.

Mirror-image perceptions: Conflicting parties view each other in the same distorted, negative ways. Each side sees the other as evil and themselves as righteous — fuels conflict cycles.

Promoting peace (LT 4.3-15):
• Contact hypothesis: Equal-status contact between groups reduces prejudice (Robbers Cave, Sherif)
• Superordinate goals: Shared goals requiring cooperation unite rival groups
• GRIT (Graduated Reciprocation in Tension-Reduction): One side makes small conciliatory gestures, inviting reciprocation — de-escalates conflict
• Mediation and communication: Structured dialogue reduces misperceptions; negotiation works better than ultimatums

4.4–4.5 Personality — Psychodynamic & Humanistic

LTs 4.4-1 · 4.5-1 through 4.5-8

Personality: An individual's characteristic pattern of thinking, feeling, and acting — relatively stable across time and situations.

Four major theoretical frameworks:
1. Psychoanalytic/Psychodynamic — unconscious drives, early childhood
2. Humanistic — conscious experience, free will, growth
3. Trait — stable, measurable characteristics (Big Five)
4. Social-cognitive — interaction of person, behavior, environment

Freud developed psychoanalysis from treating patients with hysteria — physical symptoms with no organic cause. Led him to hypothesize an unconscious mind where repressed conflicts resided.

Three levels of mind: Conscious → Preconscious → Unconscious (the "iceberg")

Structural model:
• Id: Unconscious; pleasure principle; demands immediate satisfaction; contains libido (life/sex drive) and thanatos (death/aggression drive)
• Ego: Mostly conscious; reality principle; mediates between id's demands and reality
• Superego: Internalized moral standards from parents; includes conscience (guilt) and ego-ideal (aspirations)

Psychosexual stages:

The ego uses defense mechanisms — unconscious strategies — to reduce anxiety arising from conflicts between the id, superego, and reality. See the Defense Mechanisms tab for all 12 with examples.

Most Tested Defense Mechanisms
Repression (forgetting trauma) · Projection (attributing your feelings to others) · Rationalization (self-justifying excuses) · Reaction formation (acting opposite to true feelings) · Displacement (redirecting emotion to safer target) · Sublimation (channeling impulses productively)

Neo-Freudians accepted: the importance of the unconscious, the idea that childhood shapes personality, the use of talk therapy.

Neo-Freudians rejected: sexuality as the primary driver of personality; the deterministic view of biological drives as fixed.

Criticisms of Freud:
• Concepts (id, libido) are largely untestable — hard to falsify scientifically
• Over-reliance on case studies of atypical patients
• Male and Western cultural bias
• Repression is not well-supported by memory research

What modern research supports:
• The unconscious does exist — but it's more automatic information processing than a seething cauldron of repressed conflicts
• Implicit memory, priming, and subliminal processing show unconscious influence on behavior
• Terror management theory: Fear of death (a Freudian idea) does motivate much human behavior
• Attachment research supports the importance of early relationships — but not specifically psychosexual stages

Projective tests: Personality assessments using ambiguous stimuli to elicit unconscious thoughts, feelings, and motives. The idea is that people "project" their inner world onto ambiguous material.

Rorschach Inkblot Test: 10 symmetrical inkblots; responses coded for content, form, movement. Widely used clinically.

TAT (Thematic Apperception Test): 20 ambiguous pictures; participants tell a story about each. Scored for themes of achievement, affiliation, power.

Criticisms of Projective Tests
• Low reliability: Different clinicians often score the same response differently
• Low validity: Scores often don't predict behavior as well as simpler self-report measures
• Cultural bias: Interpretations reflect the clinician's cultural framework
• Despite criticisms, they remain widely used clinically — practitioners value qualitative insight even without strong psychometric support

Humanistic psychology's view (LT 4.5-6): People are inherently good and motivated toward growth. Goal: understand the whole person from their subjective perspective. Emphasizes conscious experience, free will, and present/future — not just past trauma.

How humanists assessed self (LT 4.5-7):
• Rogers' Q-sort: Participants sort cards describing traits into piles from "most like me" to "least like me" — repeated to compare real self vs. ideal self, and changes over therapy
• Self-concept questionnaires: Direct measures of perceived self-worth and identity

Rogers' key concepts: Unconditional positive regard, congruence (real vs. ideal self), actualizing tendency, conditions of worth.

Criticisms of Humanistic Theory (LT 4.5-8)
• Concepts like "self-actualization" are vague and difficult to test
• Too optimistic — ignores genuine evil and destructive impulses
• Culturally biased toward Western individualism
• Positive influence: Inspired client-centered therapy, positive psychology, and focus on human strengths

Defense Mechanisms

LT 4.5-2 · Freud's ego strategies to manage anxiety — all heavily tested

Key Principle
All defense mechanisms are unconscious — the person is unaware of using them. They are employed by the ego to protect against anxiety from id–superego–reality conflicts.

Most tested: repression, projection, rationalization, reaction formation, displacement, sublimation. For the FRQ, always give a specific behavioral example, not just a definition.

4.6 Social-Cognitive & Trait Theories

LTs 4.6-1 through 4.6-6

Trait theorists describe personality using stable, consistent characteristics that predict behavior across situations.

Allport: Identified ~18,000 trait words. Classified traits as cardinal (one defining trait), central (5–10 characteristic patterns), secondary (situational preferences).

Eysenck: Reduced traits to two biological dimensions — Introversion/Extraversion and Neuroticism/Stability. Argued introverts have higher baseline arousal, which is why they avoid stimulation.

Personality inventories: Long questionnaires with many items, empirically scored. Strengths: standardized, reliable, efficient. Weaknesses: social desirability bias, faking, limited self-insight.

MMPI-2: 567 items; originally designed to diagnose disorders; includes validity scales to detect faking. NEO-PI-R: Measures Big Five. MBTI: Based on Jung; poor test-retest reliability.

Trait (OCEAN)High ScoreLow Score
OpennessCreative, curious, imaginativeConventional, practical, routine-oriented
ConscientiousnessOrganized, disciplined, dependableImpulsive, disorganized, careless
ExtraversionOutgoing, sociable, positive affectReserved, introverted, quiet

Conscientiousness = strongest predictor of academic and job success. Neuroticism = strongest predictor of mental health difficulties.

Research supports that personality traits are moderately to highly consistent over time and across situations — especially after age 30.

Person-situation controversy (Mischel, 1968): Argued that behavior varies too much by situation to support strong trait claims. Modern resolution: interactionist view — behavior emerges from an interaction of traits and situations. Traits predict average behavior across many situations, not specific behavior in one situation.

Longitudinal studies show Big Five traits are heritable (~50%) and remain stable from early adulthood through old age, with small increases in conscientiousness and agreeableness over the lifespan.

Reciprocal determinism (Bandura): Personality emerges from the ongoing interaction of person (cognitions, beliefs) ↔ behavior ↔ environment. Each factor influences the others.

Self-efficacy: Belief in your ability to succeed on a specific task. Sources: past performance (strongest), vicarious experience (watching models), social persuasion, physiological states. High self-efficacy → greater effort, persistence, achievement.

Locus of control (Rotter): Internal = "I control outcomes." External = "Luck/others control outcomes." Internal locus correlates with higher achievement and well-being.

Learned helplessness (Seligman): After repeated uncontrollable negative events, organisms stop trying even when escape is possible. Linked to depression.

Criticisms of Social-Cognitive Theory (LT 4.6-6)
• Too focused on cognition; may underestimate the role of emotions and unconscious factors
• Does not fully explain where personality consistency comes from
• Some argue it is better at describing behavior in specific situations than explaining overall personality

4.7 Motivation

LTs 4.7-1 through 4.7-7 · Why we do what we do

Motivation: A need or desire that energizes and directs behavior toward a goal.

TheoryCore IdeaLimitation
Instinct theoryBehavior is genetically programmed — we are motivated by fixed action patterns (William James: 37 instincts)Circular reasoning; doesn't explain variation within a species
Drive-reduction theoryPhysiological needs create arousal (drives) that motivate behavior to reduce tension and restore homeostasis (Hull)Doesn't explain curiosity or thrill-seeking; we sometimes increase arousal

Yerkes-Dodson Law
Performance is best at a moderate level of arousal. For simple tasks, higher arousal helps; for complex tasks, lower arousal is better. Explains why nervousness hurts difficult tasks but helps simple ones.

Need to belong (Baumeister & Leary): A fundamental human motivation — we need frequent, positive interactions with stable, caring relationships. This need is as basic as hunger.

Evidence for the need to belong:
• Social exclusion activates the same brain regions as physical pain (fMRI studies)
• Ostracism (Cyberball experiments) reduces feelings of belonging, control, and self-esteem immediately
• Loneliness is linked to higher mortality rates, weakened immune function, and depression
• People form bonds quickly and resist their dissolution

Social networking (LT 4.7-4):
• Online social networks fulfill the need to belong for many — especially introverts
• However, passive social media use (scrolling without interacting) is linked to reduced well-being and increased social comparison
• Active engagement (posting, messaging) shows fewer negative effects
• Fear of Missing Out (FOMO): Social media heightens awareness of others' social lives, increasing feelings of exclusion

Achievement motivation (McClelland): A desire to excel, accomplish goals, and succeed. People high in achievement motivation choose moderately challenging tasks — easy enough to succeed, hard enough to feel meaningful.

Intrinsic vs. extrinsic motivation:
• Intrinsic: Motivated by inherent interest, enjoyment, or challenge
• Extrinsic: Motivated by external rewards (grades, money, praise)
• Overjustification effect: Adding external rewards to an intrinsically motivating activity reduces intrinsic motivation ("I used to love reading — now I do it for points")

Encouraging achievement:
• Focus on mastery goals (improving skill) vs. performance goals (outperforming others)
• Attribute success to effort, not fixed ability (growth mindset, Dweck)
• Provide immediate, specific feedback
• Encourage autonomy and competence (Self-Determination Theory, Deci & Ryan)

Physiological factors (LT 4.7-6):
• Blood glucose: Low blood sugar signals hunger to the hypothalamus
• Hypothalamus: The lateral hypothalamus triggers eating; the ventromedial hypothalamus signals fullness/satiety
• Hormones: Ghrelin (stomach) rises before meals, stimulating appetite; Leptin (fat cells) signals fullness; Insulin regulates blood glucose
• Set point / settling point: The body defends a particular weight range; reducing caloric intake lowers metabolism

Cultural and situational factors (LT 4.7-7):
• Taste preferences: Shaped by culture — what is a delicacy in one culture is inedible in another
• Unit bias: We tend to eat one unit of whatever is served — larger portions = more eating
• Social facilitation of eating: We eat more in groups
• Variety effect: More food variety = more eating
• Situational cues: Bowl size, plate color, distraction (TV), time of day all affect intake

Obesity — A Biopsychosocial Issue
Genetic factors (~70% heritability of BMI), metabolic rate, food environment, activity level, psychological factors (stress, emotional eating) all contribute. No single cause; requires multifaceted interventions.

4.8 Emotions

LTs 4.8-1 through 4.8-9 · How we feel and express emotion

The core question: Do emotions arise from our physiology, our cognitions, or both — and in what order?

James-Lange Theory
Stimulus → Physiological Response → Emotion

We feel emotions because we notice our own physiological reactions. "We do not tremble because we are afraid; we are afraid because we tremble."
Cannon-Bard Theory
Stimulus → Emotion AND Physiological Response simultaneously

The thalamus simultaneously sends signals to the cortex (emotion) and body (arousal). Physiological responses and feelings occur at the same time, independently.
Schachter-Singer Two-Factor Theory
Stimulus → Physiological Arousal + Cognitive Label → Emotion

Emotion = arousal + a label we apply to explain it. The "bridge study": Aroused men on a scary bridge attributed arousal to attraction; on a safe bridge, they did not.
Lazarus Cognitive Appraisal
Stimulus → Cognitive Appraisal → Emotion

Thought must precede emotion. How we interpret an event (primary appraisal: threat or opportunity?) determines which emotion we feel.
Zajonc / LeDoux — Emotion Without Cognition
Some emotional responses occur without conscious thought

The amygdala can trigger fear responses before the cortex consciously processes the stimulus. Emotional reactions can precede and bypass deliberate cognitive appraisal.
AP Key Point (LT 4.8-2)
Both positions are partly correct. Some emotions (fear, disgust) can occur without conscious labeling (Zajonc, LeDoux). Other emotions require cognitive appraisal (Lazarus). The debate is about which pathway is primary, not which is the only one.

Paul Ekman identified 6 basic emotions recognized across cultures (evidence from isolated tribes): happiness, sadness, fear, anger, disgust, surprise. Some researchers add contempt as a seventh.

These emotions are considered universal because they appear cross-culturally, emerge early in development, and have recognizable facial expressions.

Plutchik's Wheel of Emotions: 8 primary emotions arranged in opposing pairs (joy/sadness, anger/fear, trust/disgust, anticipation/surprise); combined to create complex emotions (love = joy + trust; contempt = anger + disgust).

Autonomic nervous system (LT 4.8-4):
• Sympathetic NS: Activates during emotional arousal — accelerates heart rate, dilates pupils, inhibits digestion, increases respiration ("fight-or-flight")
• Parasympathetic NS: Restores calm after the arousal passes — decreases heart rate, constricts pupils ("rest-and-digest")

Brain structures (LT 4.8-5):
• Amygdala: Fear center; processes threatening stimuli; responds faster than conscious thought (LeDoux's "low road" — subcortical pathway for immediate fear)
• Prefrontal cortex: Regulates emotion; damage causes inappropriate emotional responses
• Left frontal lobe: Associated with positive emotions
• Right frontal lobe: Associated with negative emotions and withdrawal

Polygraph (lie detector): Measures physiological arousal (breathing, GSR, heart rate). Unreliable for detecting lies because arousal reflects many emotions — not guilt specifically. Not admissible in most courts.

Nonverbal communication (LT 4.8-6): We communicate emotions through facial expressions, gestures, posture, eye contact, touch, and tone of voice. Nonverbal cues are often more accurate indicators of true feeling than words.

Gender differences (LT 4.8-7):
• Women are generally better at both reading and expressing emotions nonverbally
• Women make more eye contact; men use more personal space
• Possible explanations: socialization (girls encouraged to be emotionally expressive), evolved caregiving roles, status differences (lower-status people read others more carefully)

Culture and gestures (LT 4.8-8):
• Gestures vary greatly across cultures — a "thumbs up" is obscene in some regions
• Personal space norms vary: Northern Europeans prefer more distance; Middle Eastern and Latin cultures less
• BUT: Facial expressions for basic emotions are culturally universal (Ekman's cross-cultural research) — people in isolated cultures recognized the same 6 facial expressions

Facial feedback hypothesis: Facial expressions do not just reflect emotions — they also influence them. Feedback from facial muscles to the brain alters the emotional experience.

Classic study (Strack et al., 1988): Participants held a pen in their mouth either with their teeth (forced smile) or with their lips (forced frown). The "smiling" group rated cartoons as funnier.

Behavioral confirmation: Acting happy (smiling, upright posture) can generate positive affect — not just reflect it. This supports use of behavioral activation in treating depression.

Botox studies: People who cannot move certain facial muscles after Botox injections show slower emotional recognition in others — suggesting expression helps us simulate others' emotions (embodied simulation).

James-Lange Connection
Facial feedback is consistent with the James-Lange theory — our bodily responses (including facial muscle movements) contribute to our subjective emotional experience, not just the reverse.

Landmark Studies

Procedure · Findings · AP implications

Milgram Obedience Study (1963)
Stanley Milgram · Yale University
Participants ordered to administer increasing shocks (15–450V) to a "learner" (confederate) by an authority figure in a lab coat.
65% went to maximum 450V. Obedience decreased with victim proximity, peer defiance, and absent authority.
AP: Demonstrates situational power over behavior; raises FAE issue; ethical landmark in research ethics.
Asch Conformity Experiments (1951)
Solomon Asch · Swarthmore College
Participants judged line lengths alongside confederates who unanimously gave wrong answers.
~37% conformity; 75% conformed at least once. One ally reduced conformity to ~5%.
AP: Normative social influence ≠ obedience. No authority figure — just peer pressure.
Stanford Prison Experiment (1971)
Philip Zimbardo · Stanford University
Students randomly assigned as "guards" or "prisoners" in a simulated prison environment.
Guards became abusive; prisoners became passive and distressed. Ended after 6 days.
AP: Situational roles shape behavior; criticized for ethical violations, coaching, and lack of scientific controls.
Bobo Doll Study (1961)
Albert Bandura · Stanford University
Children watched adults aggress against a Bobo doll — live, filmed, and cartoon versions. Then given mild frustration and play time.
Children imitated aggressive behaviors, including novel acts. Effect held for filmed models. Boys imitated more; same-sex models had stronger effect.
AP: Foundational for observational/social learning theory. Vicarious reinforcement increases imitation.
Darley & Latané Bystander Studies (1968)
Columbia University
Participants heard a seizure, saw smoke, or witnessed an emergency — alone or in groups.
85% helped when alone vs. 31% in groups. Diffusion of responsibility + pluralistic ignorance explain the effect.
AP: To increase helping — reduce group size, assign specific responsibility, eliminate ambiguity.
Festinger & Carlsmith — Cognitive Dissonance (1959)
Stanford University
Participants paid $1 or $20 to lie about a boring task; later rated their true enjoyment.
$1 group rated the task more positively. Insufficient external justification forced internal attitude change.
AP: Insufficient justification = more dissonance = more attitude change. Counter-intuitive: less reward → more persuasion of oneself.
Robbers Cave Experiment (1954)
Muzafer Sherif · Oklahoma
Boys at summer camp divided into competing groups, then given superordinate goals.
Competition created strong in-group bias. Superordinate goals (shared challenges) successfully reduced hostility.
AP: Supports contact hypothesis — cooperative, equal-status contact with shared goals reduces prejudice.
Schachter-Singer "Bridge Study" (1962)
Two-Factor Theory of Emotion
Men crossed either a scary suspension bridge or a stable bridge, then were approached by an attractive interviewer.
Men on the scary bridge were more likely to attribute their arousal to attraction (misattribution of arousal) and call the interviewer.
AP: Classic demonstration of two-factor theory — same arousal, different label = different emotion.
Strack Pen-in-Mouth Study (1988)
Fritz Strack — Facial Feedback
Participants held a pen with their teeth (inducing a smile) or lips (inducing a pout) while rating cartoons.
"Smiling" group rated cartoons as funnier, supporting the facial feedback hypothesis.
AP: LT 4.8-9 — facial expressions don't just reflect emotions; they also generate them. Consistent with James-Lange theory.

Key Terms

All major vocabulary aligned to learning targets

4.1 Attribution & Person Perception
Attribution Theory
Framework explaining how we infer the causes of behavior as internal (dispositional) or external (situational).
Fundamental Attribution Error
When judging others, overestimating dispositional causes and underestimating situational ones.
Self-Serving Bias
Attributing personal successes to internal factors and failures to external factors.
Actor-Observer Bias
We explain our behavior situationally, but others' behavior dispositionally.
Social Comparison
Evaluating our opinions and abilities by comparing ourselves to others; shapes self-perception.
Explicit Prejudice
Conscious, deliberate negative attitudes toward a group that the person is aware of and endorses.
Implicit Prejudice
Unconscious, automatic bias measured by the Implicit Association Test (IAT).
Stereotype Threat
Awareness of a negative stereotype about one's group disrupts performance on related tasks (Steele).
Just-World Phenomenon
Belief that the world is fair and people get what they deserve; leads to victim-blaming.
4.2 Attitudes
Cognitive Dissonance
Discomfort from conflicting cognitions; motivates attitude or behavior change (Festinger).
Central Route Persuasion
Attitude change through careful analysis of logical arguments; produces durable change.
Peripheral Route Persuasion
Attitude change via superficial cues (attractiveness, authority); produces temporary change.
Mere Exposure Effect
Repeated exposure to a stimulus increases liking, even without awareness.
Foot-in-the-Door
Small initial request increases likelihood of complying with a larger request later.
4.3 Social Situations
Social Contagion
Automatic spread of behaviors, emotions, or ideas through a group — a form of unconscious conformity.
Normative Social Influence
Conforming to be liked/accepted; public compliance without private acceptance.
Informational Social Influence
Conforming because we genuinely believe others have more accurate information.
Obedience
Changing behavior in direct response to a command from a perceived authority figure.
Social Facilitation
Others' presence enhances easy/practiced tasks and impairs difficult/novel tasks (Zajonc).
Social Loafing
Reduced individual effort in a group due to diffused accountability.
Deindividuation
Loss of self-awareness and restraint in group situations; leads to impulsive behavior.
Group Polarization
Group discussion amplifies the average pre-existing attitude of group members.
Groupthink
Desire for group harmony overrides critical thinking; suppresses dissent (Janis).
Frustration-Aggression Principle
Frustration (blocked goals) increases the likelihood of aggressive behavior (Dollard).
Bystander Effect
Larger number of bystanders reduces individual likelihood of helping (Darley & Latané).
Diffusion of Responsibility
Each person feels less personally accountable when others are present who could also act.
Altruism
Unselfish concern for the welfare of others; helping without expectation of personal reward.
Social Exchange Theory
We help when perceived benefits outweigh costs; helping is rational self-interest.
Superordinate Goals
Shared goals requiring cooperation between competing groups; reduces intergroup conflict.
Mirror-Image Perceptions
Conflicting parties each view themselves as righteous and the other as evil; fuels conflict.
Passionate Love
Intense, aroused longing for union; associated with novelty and idealization; peaks early.
Companionate Love
Deep, affectionate attachment based on trust and shared history; grows over time.
4.4–4.5 Personality — Psychodynamic & Humanistic
Id / Ego / Superego
Freud's three structures: unconscious drives (id), rational mediator (ego), internalized morality (superego).
Defense Mechanisms
Unconscious ego strategies to reduce anxiety (repression, projection, rationalization, etc.).
Collective Unconscious
Jung's shared, inherited reservoir of archetypes across all of humanity.
Inferiority Complex
Adler's term for deep feelings of inadequacy that motivate striving for superiority.
Unconditional Positive Regard
Rogers: full, non-judgmental acceptance of a person; promotes psychological growth and congruence.
Congruence
Rogers: match between real self and ideal self; incongruence produces anxiety and maladjustment.
Self-Actualization
Maslow's highest need — fulfilling one's greatest potential; the peak of the hierarchy.
Projective Test
Personality assessment using ambiguous stimuli (Rorschach, TAT) to reveal unconscious processes.
4.6 Traits & Social-Cognitive
Big Five (OCEAN)
Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism — the five broad personality dimensions.
Self-Efficacy
Bandura: belief in one's ability to perform specific behaviors needed to achieve goals.
Reciprocal Determinism
Bandura: personality emerges from mutual interaction of person (cognitions), behavior, and environment.
Locus of Control
Rotter: Internal = you control outcomes; External = luck/others control outcomes.
Learned Helplessness
Seligman: passive resignation after repeated uncontrollable negative events; linked to depression.
Overjustification Effect
Adding external rewards to an intrinsically motivating activity reduces intrinsic motivation.
4.7 Motivation
Drive-Reduction Theory
Physiological needs create arousal (drives) that motivate behavior to restore homeostasis (Hull).
Yerkes-Dodson Law
Performance peaks at moderate arousal; for complex tasks, lower arousal is optimal; for simple tasks, higher arousal helps.
Need to Belong
Fundamental human motivation for frequent, positive interactions with stable, caring relationships (Baumeister).
Ghrelin / Leptin
Ghrelin (stomach) stimulates appetite; Leptin (fat cells) signals fullness/satiety to the hypothalamus.
Intrinsic Motivation
Motivation driven by internal interest, enjoyment, or personal challenge — not external reward.
4.8 Emotion
James-Lange Theory
Stimulus → physiological response → emotion. We feel emotions because we notice our bodily arousal.
Cannon-Bard Theory
Stimulus → simultaneous emotion AND physiological response. Body arousal and feelings are independent, parallel processes.
Two-Factor Theory
Schachter-Singer: emotion = physiological arousal + cognitive label. Arousal can be misattributed to different emotions based on context.
Facial Feedback Hypothesis
Facial expressions not only reflect emotions — they also influence them. Smiling can make you feel happier.
Basic Emotions
Ekman's 6 universally recognized emotions: happiness, sadness, fear, anger, disgust, surprise.
Polygraph
Measures physiological arousal to detect lies; unreliable because arousal reflects many emotions, not just guilt.

Practice Quiz

18 AP-style questions covering all learning targets 4.1–4.8

Q 01 · LT 4.1-1
A manager assumes an employee missed a deadline because she is lazy, ignoring that the employee's computer broke. This illustrates:
FAE: judging others by their character (lazy) while ignoring situation (broken computer).
Q 02 · LT 4.1-3
Which best distinguishes explicit prejudice from implicit prejudice?
Explicit = aware, deliberate bias. Implicit = unconscious, automatic bias (measured by IAT — the reverse of the wrong answer D).
Q 03 · LT 4.2-2
A voter reads a detailed 10-page policy analysis before deciding whom to support. This is an example of:
Central route = careful analysis of logical arguments. Results in durable attitude change when the audience is motivated and able.
Q 04 · LT 4.3-1
People in a crowd at a comedy show begin laughing at a joke even though many didn't hear it clearly, simply because others around them are laughing. This best illustrates:
Social contagion = automatic, unconscious spread of emotions/behaviors through a group — a form of conformity without deliberate choice.
Q 05 · LT 4.3-2
Which factor most significantly REDUCED obedience in Milgram's variations of the experiment?
Victim proximity dramatically reduced obedience — when the learner was in the same room, obedience fell to 40%; when participants had to physically touch the learner, it fell to 30%.
Q 06 · LT 4.3-5
After discussing gun control in like-minded groups, those who initially mildly favored stricter laws become strongly in favor, while those mildly opposed become strongly opposed. This illustrates:
Group polarization: discussion with like-minded people strengthens pre-existing attitudes, making them more extreme.
Q 07 · LT 4.3-10
Research on interpersonal attraction consistently shows that one of the most powerful predictors of friendship formation is:
Proximity promotes familiarity (mere exposure effect). We're most likely to befriend those we encounter frequently — dorm floor neighbors, classmates, co-workers.
Q 08 · LT 4.3-11
Long-term romantic relationships typically involve a shift from __________ to __________.
Passionate love (intense, aroused, novelty-based) fades over time; companionate love (deep trust, shared history) grows. Both can coexist in healthy long-term relationships.
Q 09 · LT 4.5-2
A person who is secretly jealous of a colleague's success begins telling everyone how wonderful that colleague is. This defense mechanism is:
Reaction formation = expressing the opposite of one's true, anxiety-producing feeling. Jealousy is converted into excessive praise.
Q 10 · LT 4.5-3
Karen Horney's main criticism of Freud was that he:
Horney argued Freud's theories reflected male cultural bias — she rejected "penis envy" and proposed "womb envy" as a counter. She emphasized cultural/social roots of anxiety, not biological sex drives.
Q 11 · LT 4.6-3
Which Big Five personality trait is the strongest predictor of academic and occupational success?
Conscientiousness (organized, disciplined, dependable) is consistently the strongest Big Five predictor of academic achievement and job performance.
Q 12 · LT 4.7-1
The Yerkes-Dodson law predicts that a surgeon performing a routine procedure for the 500th time would perform BEST under:
For simple/well-practiced tasks, higher arousal is beneficial. Routine surgery is well-practiced, so moderate-to-high arousal (staying alert) enhances performance.
Q 13 · LT 4.7-3
Research on the need to belong indicates that social exclusion:
fMRI studies show ostracism (e.g., being excluded from Cyberball) activates the same brain regions as physical pain — supporting the fundamental, biological nature of the need to belong.
Q 14 · LT 4.7-5
A student who used to love painting starts receiving grades for art projects and gradually loses interest in painting for fun. This best illustrates:
The overjustification effect: adding external rewards (grades) to an intrinsically motivating activity (painting for fun) undermines intrinsic motivation.
Q 15 · LT 4.8-1
According to the two-factor theory (Schachter-Singer), which two components combine to produce an emotion?
Two-factor (Schachter-Singer): arousal + cognitive label = emotion. D describes Cannon-Bard (simultaneous); B is the correct two-factor answer.
Q 16 · LT 4.8-3
Paul Ekman's cross-cultural research supported the idea that:
Ekman showed that people in isolated tribal cultures (no TV or media exposure) recognized the same 6 basic emotions from facial photographs — strong evidence for biologically universal expressions.
Q 17 · LT 4.8-8
Which aspect of nonverbal communication varies significantly across cultures, while another is relatively universal?
Gestures (thumbs up, OK sign) vary greatly by culture — some are offensive in different regions. But basic facial expressions (Ekman's 6) are recognized universally.
Q 18 · LT 4.8-9
The "pen-in-mouth" study by Strack supports which theory?
Facial feedback: forcing facial muscles into a smile (pen between teeth) increased enjoyment of cartoons — facial expressions influence emotions, not just reflect them.
